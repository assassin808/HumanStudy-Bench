# Study 006: A Room with a Viewpoint - Using Social Norms to Motivate Environmental Conservation in Hotels

## Citation
Goldstein, N. J., Cialdini, R. B., & Griskevicius, V. (2008). A room with a viewpoint: Using social norms to motivate environmental conservation in hotels. *Journal of Consumer Research*, 35(3), 472-482.

## Overview
This field experiment examined the effectiveness of different types of messages in motivating hotel guests to participate in an environmental conservation program (towel reuse). The study demonstrated that descriptive normative messages (informing guests that most others participate) significantly outperform traditional environmental protection messages.

## Key Phenomenon

**Social Norm Effect**: Descriptive normative information (what most people do) is more effective at motivating prosocial behavior than appeals focusing solely on environmental protection.

**Provincial Norm Effect**: Norms of one's immediate surroundings (e.g., same hotel room) are more effective than norms of less immediate surroundings (e.g., whole hotel), even when provincial norms are rationally no more diagnostic of appropriate behavior.

**Core Findings**:
- **Experiment 1**: Descriptive norm message (44.1%) significantly outperformed environmental message (35.1%)
- **Experiment 2**: Same room identity (provincial norm) achieved highest participation rate (49.3%)
- All descriptive norm messages combined (44.5%) significantly outperformed environmental message (37.2%)

## Experimental Design

### Between-Subjects Manipulation

**Experiment 1** (2 conditions):
- **Environmental Condition**: Traditional message focusing on environmental protection
- **Descriptive Norm Condition**: Message informing guests that 75% of other guests participate

**Experiment 2** (5 conditions):
- **Environmental Condition**: Traditional environmental protection message
- **Descriptive Norm - Guest Identity**: Norms of hotel guests (global norm)
- **Descriptive Norm - Same Room Identity**: Norms of guests who stayed in the same room (provincial norm)
- **Descriptive Norm - Citizen Identity**: Norms of citizens (meaningful identity)
- **Descriptive Norm - Gender Identity**: Norms of men/women (meaningful identity)

### Procedure

1. **Setting**: Mid-sized, mid-priced hotel in the Southwest (part of national chain)
2. **Intervention**: Towel reuse signs placed on bathroom towel racks
3. **Message Assignment**: Each room randomly assigned to one message condition
4. **Data Collection**: Room attendants recorded whether guests reused at least one towel on their first eligible day
5. **Participants**: Hotel guests staying minimum 2 nights (not aware of study participation)

### Materials

- **Towel Reuse Signs**: Professionally printed, laminated signs with condition-specific messages
- **Instructions**: Clear instructions on how to participate (drape towels on rack) or not participate (place on floor)
- **Impact Information**: Back of card with environmental impact statistics

## Key Measures

### Primary Measure
- **Towel Reuse Rate**: Proportion of guests who reused at least one towel during their first eligible day

### Secondary Measures
- Comparison between message conditions
- Provincial norm effect (same room vs other conditions)
- Descriptive norm effect (all descriptive norms vs environmental)

## Original Results

### Experiment 1
- **Environmental Condition**: 35.1% towel reuse rate (76/216)
- **Descriptive Norm Condition**: 44.1% towel reuse rate (96/217)
- **Statistical Test**: χ²(1) = 3.72, p = 0.05, φ = 0.09

### Experiment 2
- **Environmental Condition**: 37.2% (119/319)
- **Descriptive Norm - Guest**: 44.0% (140/319)
- **Descriptive Norm - Same Room**: 49.3% (157/319) ⭐ Highest
- **Descriptive Norm - Citizen**: 43.5% (139/319)
- **Descriptive Norm - Gender**: 40.9% (130/319)
- **All Descriptive Norms Combined**: 44.5% (568/1276)

### Key Statistical Findings
- Descriptive norms significantly outperformed environmental message (χ²(1) = 4.94, p = 0.03)
- Same room identity (provincial norm) significantly outperformed other descriptive norm conditions (χ²(1) = 4.18, p < 0.05)

## Theoretical Implications

1. **Descriptive Norms**: Simply informing people what others do can be more effective than appeals to values or environmental protection
2. **Provincial Norms**: Situational similarity (same room) can be more influential than personal identity similarity (citizen, gender)
3. **Social Identity**: Making a meaningful identity salient without descriptive normative information is not optimal

## Practical Implications

- Hotels and organizations can increase conservation program participation by using descriptive normative appeals
- Provincial norms (most local/relevant setting) are most effective
- Messages should reference norms of the most immediate setting possible
- Social science research and theory should guide persuasive appeals rather than intuition

## Replication Notes

- Effect is robust in real-world field settings
- Provincial norm effect demonstrates power of situational similarity
- Important to ensure descriptive norms are true and consistent with desired behavior direction
- Effect may vary with individual differences in environmental concern or social identity importance
